New Orleans’ eclectic mix of people and cultures, cemented through centuries of immigration and imperialism, has left a vivid feast of culinary traditions and unique dishes that make for foodie heaven. Relish the spicy Creole flavours born out of a historic mix of French, African, Spanish and plenty more, and tuck-in to the blue-collar creations like the Poor Boy, born out of starving and striking workers that local deli’s and cafes would feed free of charge. For it is in the food that we find the story of Nola’s unique identity, and its strong sense of community.

New Orleans has been a pivotal influencer on the bar scene across the world. From the Sazerac to the Vieux Carré, New Orleans has fostered the cocktail in a mixture of high-end hotel bars and down and out drinking dens, ultimately popularising it. For an authentic taste of old-world cocktail flair, and to stomp the same grounds as Ernest Hemingway, William Faulkner, Tennessee Williams and Truman Capote, explore the bars of the Crescent City.

Jazz, grandfather to all popular music, was fostered in New Orleans and expressed the jubilant, playful nature of the city. A tapestry of cultural influences came together to create the style; sharp European brass mixed with African Voodoo drumming created a heady, rhythmic sound that promoted swing and improv. Slaves under Spanish occupation were permitted Sunday’s off, so they gathered in Congo Square to beat the heat, socialise, and play music. This was suppressed under American rule, but the practice stuck allowing African-American rhythms to persevere. Mix the liberal attitudes brought about by a joyous mix of people, plus a penchant for making music morning, day and night, and the perfect storm brews.

As a strategic southern outpost, New Orleans has changed hands many times, offering fluxes of people, languages and cultures. This has created a dynamic and rich history, which spoils any visitor with hints and glimpses of this illustrious past. From the architecture of the French Quarter, the unique parades of Mardi Gras and Treme Second Lines, or the Voodoo rituals and gris-gris sellers, you’ll be privy to startlingly idiosyncratic traditions and styles that seep in the collective history of this enigmatic city.

The coffee is uniquely brewed with chicory, channeling the Creole’s resourcefulness during the American Civil War where coffee was very limited.
